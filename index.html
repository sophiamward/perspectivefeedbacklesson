Micro‑Lesson

Key concepts (Abernethy) & Cognitive Flexibility

Perspective‑Taking (Abernethy’s Four‑Step Technique)
  1. Seek Understanding: Intentionally set aside your own goals; center the other person’s lived experience.
  2. Ideate: Imagine several ways they might be seeing the situation; note obstacles and possibilities.
  3. Hypothesize: Narrow to your best testable guess(es) about their perspective and expected outcomes.
  4. Observe & Adjust: Engage, listen, and revise your assumptions with curiosity.
Cognitive Flexibility is the ability to shift mental sets, consider alternative explanations, and adapt behavior when context changes—without becoming rigid or defensive. It supports learning, performance, and mental health and is linked to lower burnout risk in healthcare settings.
Why it matters in medicine: reduces diagnostic anchoring, improves patient rapport and adherence, enhances team learning, and protects wellness under stress.

Quick clinical‑education examples

Rigid / defensive response

Context: Anatomy lab TA notes your dissection landmarks are off. You think, “But I followed the atlas.”

Reply: “I did it the way the book says. I don’t see the issue.”

  • No effort to seek the TA’s perspective
  • Anchors on one source; learning stalls
  • Stress rises → risk of frustration/burnout
Curious / adaptive response

Context: Same scenario.

Reply: “Could you show me what you’re seeing? I may be anchoring on the atlas image. If I’m off, how can I adjust my approach for tomorrow’s lab?”

  • Centers the TA’s view → specific, actionable input
  • Adapts strategy; learning accelerates
  • Psychological flexibility → steadier mood & efficacy
Tip: You’ll unlock progress as you go.

Check your understanding

1) Step 1 of Abernethy’s technique (“Seek Understanding”) means…

Correct! Centering the other person comes first; your goals pause.
Not quite. Step 1 is about temporarily letting go of your goals and focusing on their perspective.

2) Cognitive flexibility is best described as…

Yes. Flexibility = noticing, shifting, and adapting—not people‑pleasing.
Try again. Flexibility is about adapting thinking/behavior when conditions change.

3) Which response shows cognitive flexibility in a team‑based learning session?

Exactly. It notices possible bias, asks for evidence, and invites adjustment.
Look for language that stays curious, seeks data, and leaves room to change.
Practice Scenario

Draft course/instructor feedback using perspective‑taking + flexibility

Scenario: A pre‑clinical pharmacology lecture moved quickly, slides were dense, and exam items emphasized mechanisms not covered explicitly. Draft feedback you might submit to the course director/instructor. Use the four steps to scaffold your message, then generate a concise feedback note.

Uses respectful “I” statements more than accusatory “you”.
Signals curiosity (e.g., “help me understand…”, “I’m wondering…”, questions included).
Includes specifics (e.g., timing, slide #, example) to make feedback actionable.
Invites collaboration or next step (e.g., open to adjustments / conversation).
